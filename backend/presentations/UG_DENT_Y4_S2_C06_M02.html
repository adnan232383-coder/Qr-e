## Pediatric Dentistry II – Module: Clinical Applications ### Video Lecture Script (Approx. 12 minutes / ~1800 words) --- ## Introduction & Learning Objectives Welcome to Pediatric Dentistry II, Module:
Clinical Applications. In this session, we’ll move beyond theory and focus on practical decision-making using realistic clinical scenarios and case studies. Pediatric dentistry often feels straightforward—until you combine a child’s
behavior, parent expectations, growth and development, caries risk, trauma, and time constraints. Clinical success comes from recognizing patterns, choosing appropriate interventions, and communicating clearly. By the end of this lecture,
you should be able to: 1. Apply a structured approach to pediatric case assessment: history, risk, diagnosis, and plan. 2. Select appropriate preventive, restorative, and behavior guidance strategies for common
scenarios. 3. Manage clinical cases involving early childhood caries, deep caries in primary molars, trauma, and space management. 4. Identify key decision points—when to monitor, when to intervene, and when
to refer. 5. Communicate effectively with caregivers using simple, supportive language. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 1: A Practical Framework for Pediatric Clinical Decisions Before we jump into cases, let’s establish
a simple, repeatable workflow you can use chairside. ### Step 1: Gather focused information - Chief complaint—whose complaint is it: child, caregiver, or clinician? - Medical and dental history -
Caries risk assessment—diet, fluoride exposure, past caries, socioeconomic factors - Behavior and cooperation level - Clinical exam and radiographs as indicated ### Step 2: Define the diagnosis clearly Try to
label problems using specific terms: - [EMPHASIS] Early childhood caries - [EMPHASIS] Reversible vs. irreversible pulpitis (as best as you can determine in primary teeth) - [EMPHASIS] Incipient lesions vs.
cavitated lesions - [EMPHASIS] Traumatic dental injury classification: uncomplicated crown fracture, luxation, avulsion, etc. ### Step 3: Choose the plan that fits the child—not just the tooth In pediatrics, “best
dentistry” is dentistry that is: - Safe - Durable - Age-appropriate - Behavior-appropriate - Prevention-centered ### Step 4: Communicate and document Make sure the caregiver understands: - What you found
- Why it matters - What you recommend now - What happens if you delay treatment **Quick summary:** A structured workflow reduces missed details and makes your treatment planning more
consistent. **Key point:** The plan is based on the child’s overall risk and cooperation—not just a single lesion. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 2: Case Study 1 — Early Childhood Caries
(ECC) in a 3-Year-Old ### Scenario A 3-year-old presents with the parent reporting: “Her front teeth are turning brown.” The child is shy, reluctant to open. The parent reports bedtime
bottle use “sometimes,” and frequent snacking. No established dental home. ### Findings - White spot lesions and brown cavitations on maxillary primary incisors - Plaque accumulation along gingival margins -
Caries risk: high - Behavior: limited cooperation today ### Clinical decision points This is classic [EMPHASIS] ECC: rapid progression, high-risk environment, and often limited cooperation. Your priorities: 1. Stop disease
progression 2. Reduce pain and infection risk 3. Establish a prevention plan with caregiver buy-in 4. Choose treatment that’s feasible with current behavior ### Treatment options (tailored) - **Immediate prevention:**
- Fluoride varnish today - Caregiver counseling: bedtime bottle elimination, limit sugary snacks, water between meals - Home care: smear layer of fluoridated toothpaste twice daily with caregiver help -
**Stabilization strategies:** - [EMPHASIS] Silver diamine fluoride (SDF) for cavitated lesions if cooperation is limited - Interim therapeutic restorations (ITR) if you can isolate reasonably - **Definitive restorations (when feasible):**
- For incisors: resin restorations or full coverage crowns depending on lesion extent - For severe ECC and multiple teeth: consider sedation or general anesthesia—especially if there is pain, infection,
or extensive treatment needs ### Communication script (caregiver-friendly) “Right now, your child has cavities that are active. The good news is we can stop them, but it will take teamwork.
Today we’ll apply a medicine to slow and stop cavities, and we’ll make a home plan that fits your routine.” ### Summary & Key Points - ECC is a disease
process—not isolated “bad teeth.” - [EMPHASIS] Behavior and risk level shape your treatment approach. - Stabilization (SDF/ITR) can be the bridge to definitive care. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 3: Case
Study 2 — Deep Caries in a Primary Molar: Pulp Therapy vs. Extraction ### Scenario A 6-year-old presents with intermittent pain when eating on the lower left. Parent says: “It
comes and goes.” Child is cooperative. ### Findings - Deep occlusal caries on primary mandibular second molar - Bitewing shows lesion approaching pulp; no furcation radiolucency - No facial swelling
- Percussion: mild sensitivity - Mobility: normal for age - Medical history: noncontributory ### Clinical decision points You need to decide: restoration alone, [EMPHASIS] pulpotomy, pulpectomy, or extraction. Ask: -
Is pain spontaneous or provoked? - Any night pain? - Any swelling or sinus tract? - Radiographic signs: furcation involvement, periapical pathology, pathological root resorption? ### Likely diagnosis If the
pain is provoked and intermittent without swelling and radiographs show no pathology, consider [EMPHASIS] reversible pulpitis or early irreversible changes—common gray zone in primary teeth. ### Treatment plan If caries
removal results in pulp exposure or signs indicate vital radicular pulp: - **Pulpotomy + stainless steel crown (SSC)** is often the durable choice. If there is evidence of necrosis or
radicular pathology: - **Pulpectomy** may be considered if tooth is strategic and restorable, roots are not excessively resorbed, and follow-up is realistic. If tooth is non-restorable, infection present, or cooperation/follow-up
is uncertain: - **Extraction** with appropriate space management. ### Clinical “real-world” considerations - SSC is not “over-treatment” for a heavily carious primary molar. It’s the restoration with best longevity in
high caries risk children. - The child’s caries risk suggests you should expect future lesions—so durability matters. **Summary & Key Points** - Don’t choose pulp therapy based on depth alone—use
symptoms + radiographs + clinical findings. - [EMPHASIS] Stainless steel crowns are the workhorse of pediatric restorative dentistry. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 4: Case Study 3 — Trauma: Avulsed Permanent
Incisor in a 9-Year-Old ### Scenario A 9-year-old arrives 30 minutes after a fall at school. The maxillary right central incisor is missing. The parent brings the tooth in a
tissue. Child is crying but alert. ### First question: What’s the tooth? At age 9, maxillary central incisors are permanent. This is a time-sensitive emergency. ### Key emergency priorities 1.
Confirm avulsion of a [EMPHASIS] permanent tooth 2. Assess airway, head injury, and soft tissue lacerations 3. Manage pain and anxiety 4. Replant quickly if appropriate ### The critical detail:
storage medium A tissue is dry—this is harmful. The periodontal ligament cells desiccate quickly. ### Clinical approach If the tooth is a permanent incisor and there are no contraindications: -
Rinse gently if dirty (avoid scrubbing root) - Replant immediately - Flexible splint - Provide systemic antibiotics if indicated per guidelines - Tetanus status check - Follow-up for endodontic considerations
depending on apex maturity ### Communication “Time matters with a knocked-out permanent tooth. Our goal is to place it back quickly and stabilize it, then monitor healing over time.” ###
Summary & Key Points - [EMPHASIS] Avulsion of a permanent tooth is a true dental emergency. - Avoid dry storage—ideal is milk, saline, or specialized solution; best is immediate replantation.
[PAUSE] --- ## Section 5: Case Study 4 — Space Management: Early Loss of a Primary Molar ### Scenario A 7-year-old had primary mandibular first molar extracted due to infection.
Parent asks: “Do we need anything now?” ### Clinical decision points Space maintenance depends on: - Child’s dental age and eruption status - Which tooth was lost - Crowding/spacing -
Time since extraction - Presence and position of the first permanent molar and premolars ### Likely outcome Early loss of a primary molar can allow mesial drift of the first
permanent molar and loss of arch length—especially in the mandible. ### Options - If the permanent first molar is erupted and you need to maintain space: - [EMPHASIS] Band-and-loop space
maintainer is a common choice for unilateral primary molar loss. - If multiple teeth are missing or more complex patterns exist: - Consider lower lingual holding arch (after permanent incisors
erupt) or more comprehensive orthodontic evaluation. ### Important counseling Space maintainers are not “set and forget.” Explain: - They require hygiene - They need periodic checks - They can break
or decement **Summary & Key Points** - Space management is timing-sensitive. - The decision should consider eruption stage and crowding risk, not just the extraction itself. [PAUSE] --- ## Section
6: Putting It Into Practice — Chairside “If-Then” Thinking Let’s translate these cases into practical mental shortcuts. ### If caries risk is high… - Emphasize prevention, frequent recall, fluoride varnish
- Choose durable restorations ([EMPHASIS] SSC) - Consider SDF/ITR for stabilization if cooperation is limited ### If the child’s cooperation is limited today… - Prioritize disease control and pain relief
