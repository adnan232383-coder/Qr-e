2) A Practical Framework for Any Operative Case

Before we jump into cases, let’s establish a consistent clinical framework. Think of it as a mental checklist you can ap

Step 1: Patient-Level Factors

Caries risk: low, moderate, or high

Diet and oral hygiene habits

Salivary flow and medication history

Esthetic expectations and financial considerations

Step 2: Tooth-Level Factors

Lesion type: primary caries vs recurrent, cavitated vs non-cavitated

Remaining tooth structure: enamel quality, cusp support, crack lines

Location: anterior vs posterior, cervical vs occlusal, proximal involvement

Occlusion: heavy contacts, parafunction, guidance patterns

Step 3: Clinical Feasibility

Isolation: can you place a rubber dam? subgingival margin? bleeding control?

Access and visibility: second molars, rotated teeth, tight contacts

Margin location: enamel vs dentin/cementum

Patient tolerance: gag reflex, limited opening, time constraints

Step 4: Choose an Evidence-Informed Plan

This is where you decide whether to do:

Non-operative management (remineralization, sealants, resin infiltration)

Direct restoration (composite, glass ionomer, RMGI)

Indirect restoration (inlay/onlay/crown) when structure or occlusion demands it

Pause

Always evaluate patient risk, tooth structure, and isolation before choosing a material.

Don’t start by asking “What restoration do I want to place?” Start by asking “[EMPHASIS] What does this tooth need to survive long-term?”

Scenario

A 52-year-old patient complains of sensitivity to cold on #11 and #12. You see cervical lesions—shallow, saucer-shaped.

Clinical Question

Are these lesions caries, erosion/abrasion, or abfraction-related non-carious cervical lesions (NCCLs)? And do you resto

Decision-Making

If the lesion is non-carious and asymptomatic, you may manage with desensitizing agents and behavior change.

If sensitivity persists, margins are progressing, or the patient demands esthetics—restoration is reasonable.

Material Considerations

Cervical margins often extend onto dentin/cementum where bonding is less predictable and moisture control is harder. Mat

**Resin-modified glass ionomer (RMGI):** Better moisture tolerance and chemical adhesion; good for root surface margins.

**Composite resin:** Better esthetics and polish, but requires excellent isolation and adhesive technique.

A “sandwich” approach can be used in select cases.

Clinical Technique Tips

1. **Isolation:** Retraction cord or paste can help. Control sulcular fluid and bleeding.

2. **Minimal prep:** Often no drill needed beyond cleaning and roughening; create a clean surface.

3. **Adhesion:** If using composite, consider selective enamel etch; avoid over-drying dentin.

4. **Contour:** Cervical restorations fail when over-contoured—this traps plaque and irritates gingiva.

Pause

Diagnose the cause of cervical lesions before restoring.

Choose materials based on isolation and margin location, not just esthetics.

7) Integrating Practice: A Chairside “If–Then” Tool

Let’s turn these cases into a quick clinical reasoning tool:

If the patient is **high caries risk**, then prioritize disease control, fluoride strategies, and consider materials with fluoride release where appropriate.

If the margin is **subgingival or hard to isolate**, then consider RMGI or adjust the plan to improve isolation before placing composite.

If the lesion is **deep**, then manage pulpal risk—selective removal and a strong seal.
