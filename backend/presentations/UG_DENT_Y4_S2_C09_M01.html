## Introduction (0:00–0:45) Welcome to the Restorative Dentistry Clinic course. In this module—**Introduction and Fundamentals**—we’ll build the foundation you’ll use in every clinical session: how to think about restoring teeth
predictably, conservatively, and safely. Restorative dentistry isn’t just “placing fillings.” It’s a decision-making process: diagnosing disease, controlling risk factors, choosing the right restoration, executing it with sound technique, and evaluating
outcomes over time. [PAUSE] ### Learning Objectives By the end of this lecture, you should be able to: 1. Define essential [EMPHASIS] key terms in restorative dentistry. 2. Explain core

and defective restorations. 5. Identify common pitfalls early—before they become failures. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 1: What “Restorative Dentistry” Means in Clinic (0:45–2:15) Restorative dentistry sits at the intersection of
prevention, diagnosis, and rehabilitation. In the clinic, your goal is to restore **form, function, comfort, and aesthetics**—while preserving tooth structure and protecting pulp and periodontium. A key mindset shift: the
restoration is not the treatment by itself. The restoration is often the **final step** after controlling the disease process, like caries activity, erosive wear, or parafunction. If the cause remains,
the restoration is likely to fail—sometimes quickly. [PAUSE] ### Clinical Example A patient presents with recurrent caries around a Class II composite placed one year ago. If you simply replace
the composite, but don’t address high caries risk—frequent sugar intake, low fluoride exposure, and dry mouth—the new restoration is being placed into the same failing environment. The result is a
“restoration replacement cycle,” where each replacement becomes larger and more invasive. **Key point:** manage the cause, not just the consequence. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 2: Key Terms You Must Speak
Fluently (2:15–5:10) Let’s define the terms that come up constantly in the restorative clinic. Listen for how these terms shape your clinical decisions. ### 1) [EMPHASIS] Dental Caries: Disease vs.
Lesion - **[EMPHASIS] Caries disease** is the biofilm-mediated, sugar-driven process. - A **caries lesion** is the clinical result—demineralized enamel or dentin. Why this matters: early lesions may be managed non-operatively,

and preventive plan. [PAUSE] ### 3) [EMPHASIS] Indications and Contraindications An **indication** is a reason to do a procedure; a **contraindication** is a reason not to—at least not now. Example:
A deep carious lesion close to pulp in a symptomatic tooth may contraindicate a direct restoration without pulpal assessment or endodontic planning. [PAUSE] ### 4) [EMPHASIS] Direct vs. Indirect Restorations
- **[EMPHASIS] Direct**: placed chairside in one visit, like composite or amalgam (where still used). - **[EMPHASIS] Indirect**: fabricated outside the mouth, then bonded or cemented—like inlays, onlays, crowns. Direct
is often more conservative and cost-effective; indirect can provide better control of contacts, occlusion, and cuspal coverage in larger defects. [PAUSE] ### 5) [EMPHASIS] Margins, Contacts, and Contours - **Margins**:

Section 3: Basic Principles That Drive Every Restoration (5:35–9:45) Now let’s cover the fundamental principles you should actively think through during treatment. ### Principle 1: [EMPHASIS] Minimal Intervention Dentistry The
modern goal is to preserve tooth structure. This includes: - preventing disease progression, - remineralizing early lesions when appropriate, - selective caries removal rather than aggressive excavation near pulp, -
repairing restorations when possible instead of replacing them. **Clinical note:** A repair may be more conservative and reduces risk of pulpal exposure compared with complete replacement. [PAUSE] ### Principle 2:
[EMPHASIS] Biological Respect Every restoration must respect: - the **pulp** (avoid iatrogenic trauma, manage deep caries wisely), - the **periodontium** (avoid overhangs, respect biologic width, ensure cleansability), - the **occlusion**
(avoid high points and interferences that fracture restorations or teeth). A technically beautiful restoration that ignores biology can still fail clinically. [PAUSE] ### Principle 3: [EMPHASIS] Diagnosis Before Drill Before
you pick up a handpiece, you must know: - What is the diagnosis? caries, fracture, wear, defective restoration, hypersensitivity? - Is it active disease? - Is operative treatment indicated now?
Use clinical exam, bitewing radiographs when indicated, transillumination, and caries detection methods appropriately. Avoid “restoring radiographs.” Treat the patient, not the image. [PAUSE] ### Principle 4: [EMPHASIS] Isolation and Moisture
Control Moisture control affects: - bonding strength, - marginal seal, - postoperative sensitivity, - longevity. When possible, use rubber dam. When rubber dam is not feasible, optimize isolation with high-volume
suction, retraction, cotton roll isolation, and careful patient positioning—but recognize limitations. **Key point:** contamination with saliva or blood during bonding can dramatically reduce bond strength. [PAUSE] ### Principle 5: [EMPHASIS]
Adhesive Strategy and Material Selection You’ll typically choose between: - composite resin (adhesive, aesthetic, technique sensitive), - glass ionomer or resin-modified glass ionomer (fluoride release, moisture tolerant, lower strength), -
indirect ceramic or composite options (strength and morphology, increased cost and complexity). Material selection depends on: - lesion location (enamel vs dentin, supragingival vs subgingival), - occlusal load and parafunction,
- aesthetics, - ability to isolate, - size of defect and need for cuspal coverage. [PAUSE] ### Principle 6: [EMPHASIS] Anatomy is Not Optional Occlusal anatomy, proximal contact tightness, and
emergence profile affect: - chewing efficiency, - periodontal health, - patient comfort, - risk of food trapping and caries recurrence. In posterior restorations, aim for stable contacts and proper marginal
ridge height. In anterior restorations, manage translucency, value, and surface texture to avoid a “flat” artificial look. [PAUSE] --- ## Case Scenario #1: Moderate Caries, High Risk (9:45–10:55) Let’s apply
these principles. A 22-year-old patient presents with sensitivity to cold on the lower left. Bitewing shows a radiolucency into dentin on the distal of the first molar. Clinically, there is
plaque accumulation and frequent snacking. The patient reports drinking sweetened coffee throughout the day. **Decision-making flow:** 1. Diagnose: dentinal caries likely active. 2. Risk: high—diet pattern, plaque, probable low fluoride
exposure. 3. Plan: restore the cavitated lesion, but also implement caries control—diet counseling, fluoride toothpaste, possibly fluoride varnish, and shorter recall. 4. Technique priorities: excellent isolation, caries removal strategy appropriate
for depth, and a restoration with good margins and contacts. If you restore without addressing risk, recurrence is likely at the margins within a short time window. **Key point:** in
high-risk patients, a good restoration is necessary—but not sufficient. [PAUSE] --- ## Case Scenario #2: Defective Restoration—Repair or Replace? (10:55–11:55) A 38-year-old patient has an existing composite on an upper
premolar. There’s localized marginal staining and a small chipped area, but no soft dentin, no radiographic caries, and the patient is low caries risk. Options: - **Repair**: roughen the composite,
clean, selectively etch, apply adhesive, add composite, finish and polish. - **Replace**: remove everything and redo. In many cases, **repair** is more conservative, reduces pulpal risk, saves chair time, and
preserves tooth structure—especially when the defect is localized and caries is not present. **Key point:** replacement is not always the best dentistry; sometimes it’s just the most familiar. [PAUSE] ---
