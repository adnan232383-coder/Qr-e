3) Key Terms You Must Own

Let’s define the terms that show up constantly in clinic, exams, and treatment planning.

3.1 Disease and Lesion Language

[EMPHASIS]Caries[/EMPHASIS]: a biofilm-mediated, diet-modulated, multifactorial disease leading to net mineral loss.

[EMPHASIS]Caries lesion[/EMPHASIS]: the clinical sign of the disease—such as a white spot or cavitation.

[EMPHASIS]Active[/EMPHASIS] vs [EMPHASIS]arrested[/EMPHASIS] lesion: active lesions are progressing; arrested lesions are stable and may appear shiny and hard.

3.2 Tooth Tissue Terms

[EMPHASIS]Enamel[/EMPHASIS]: highly mineralized, brittle; bonds predictably with acid etching.

[EMPHASIS]Dentin[/EMPHASIS]: tubular, vital, more elastic; bonding is more technique-sensitive.

[EMPHASIS]Pulp-dentin complex[/EMPHASIS]: dentin is not inert—cutting dentin affects pulpal health.

3.3 Restoration and Preparation Terms

[EMPHASIS]Direct restoration[/EMPHASIS]: placed directly in the mouth (e.g., composite, glass ionomer).

[EMPHASIS]Indirect restoration[/EMPHASIS]: fabricated outside the mouth (e.g., inlay, onlay, crown).

[EMPHASIS]Cavity preparation[/EMPHASIS]: shaping tooth structure to receive a restoration.

[EMPHASIS]Outline form[/EMPHASIS]: the external boundary of the preparation.

5) Overview Workflow: From Diagnosis to Follow-Up

Now let’s walk through an organized operative sequence. Think of this as your mental checklist for every procedure.

Step 1 — Assessment and Diagnosis

Chief complaint, history

Clinical exam and radiographs

Caries risk evaluation

Decide: preventive, restorative, or both

Step 2 — Treatment Planning and Consent

Your plan should include:

Restorative options and why you’re recommending one

Risks: sensitivity, fracture, recurrent caries, need for future endodontics

Alternatives: monitoring, sealants, fluoride therapy, indirect restorations

Step 3 — Anesthesia and Isolation

Comfort matters—because patient movement and stress increase errors. Isolation is chosen based on procedure and site, wi

Step 4 — Caries Removal and Tooth Preparation

Remove infected tissue appropriately

Preserve affected dentin when indicated

Establish outline suitable for material and lesion extent

Create sound margins and accessible areas for finishing

Step 5 — Matrix, Wedges, and Contact Formation (when needed)

Especially in Class II restorations:

Matrix adaptation controls contour and marginal seal

Wedging can improve gingival adaptation and contact tightness

Burnishing or sectional matrices can improve proximal contacts

Step 6 — Adhesion / Placement / Polymerization

Etch (selective enamel etch or total-etch depending on system and strategy)

Prime/bond (follow manufacturer instructions precisely)

Incremental placement when appropriate

Adequate curing: intensity, time, angulation, and access

Step 7 — Finishing, Polishing, and Occlusal Adjustment

Check occlusion in centric and excursions

Smooth margins and refine anatomy

Polish to reduce plaque retention and staining

Confirm floss passes with proper contact

Step 8 — Post-op Instructions and Recall

Explain possible transient sensitivity

Reinforce home care and risk reduction

Monitor high-risk patients more frequently

Pause

**Key point:** Operative dentistry doesn’t end when the curing light turns off. Follow-up is part of treatment.
