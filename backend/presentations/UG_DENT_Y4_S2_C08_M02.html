## Oral Surgery II — Module: Clinical Applications ### Video Lecture Script (≈12 minutes) --- ## 1) Introduction & Learning Objectives Welcome to Oral Surgery II, Module: Clinical Applications. In
this session, we’ll move from “what to do” into “how to think”—using real-world clinical scenarios to sharpen decision-making, communication, and procedural planning. Oral surgery is rarely about a single tooth
in isolation. Each patient brings unique anatomy, medical history, anxiety level, and expectations. Clinical excellence means combining surgical skill with structured reasoning. By the end of this lecture, you should
be able to: 1. Use a practical framework to evaluate oral surgery cases from intake to follow-up. 2. Identify red flags in medical history that change your surgical plan. 3.
Apply decision-making to common scenarios: extractions, third molars, infection, anticoagulation, and postoperative complications. 4. Communicate risk, obtain informed consent, and give effective post-op instructions. 5. Recognize when to treat, when
to delay, and when to refer. [PAUSE] --- ## 2) A Practical Framework for Clinical Decision-Making Let’s start with a simple structure you can use in almost every case. Think
of it as “S-A-F-E-R”: - **S**: Symptoms and Story — chief complaint, onset, severity, triggers - **A**: Anatomy and Imaging — clinical exam plus radiographs/CBCT when indicated - **F**: Fitness
for surgery — medical history, medications, ASA status, vitals - **E**: Execution plan — anesthesia choice, instruments, flap design, sectioning, hemostasis - **R**: Recovery plan — post-op meds, instructions, follow-up,
complication prevention If you consistently run through S-A-F-E-R, you reduce missed details and improve outcomes. **Key point:** Surgical difficulty often isn’t the extraction—it’s everything around it: systemic risk, infection control,
and aftercare. [PAUSE] --- ## 3) Case Study 1 — Simple Extraction That Isn’t Simple ### Scenario A 48-year-old patient presents with a “loose tooth.” Tooth #30 has severe periodontal
bone loss and class III mobility. The patient wants it “out today.” Medical history: hypertension controlled with amlodipine. No allergies. Vitals stable. ### Walkthrough Using S-A-F-E-R **Symptoms/Story:** The patient reports
occasional discomfort but mostly annoyance and difficulty chewing. **Anatomy/Imaging:** Periapical radiograph shows advanced bone loss, no clear periapical radiolucency. Furcation involvement. **Fitness:** ASA II. No major concerns. **Execution Plan:** It’s
tempting to call this a “quick extraction,” but consider: the tooth is mobile, yet the gingival tissues may be inflamed and fragile. Atraumatic technique matters—especially if future implant planning is
likely. Practical approach: - Profound local anesthesia, consider PDL injection if needed. - **[EMPHASIS]Atraumatic extraction**: gentle luxation, avoid excessive buccal plate pressure. - Curette granulation tissue carefully. - If implant
is a future option: consider socket preservation discussion now. **Recovery Plan:** Because periodontally compromised sites often ooze, ensure hemostasis: - Gauze pressure, consider hemostatic agent if persistent bleeding. - Clear
post-op instructions. ### Mini-teaching moment: Consent language A good consent includes what patients care about: - “We expect a routine extraction, but risks include prolonged bleeding, infection, swelling, and in
lower molars, rare nerve irritation.” **Summary & Key Points** - “Easy” teeth can still have complex restorative implications. - Always consider the next step: ridge preservation, prosthetic plan, and patient
expectations. - Use **[EMPHASIS]atraumatic technique** to preserve bone and soft tissue. [PAUSE] --- ## 4) Case Study 2 — Third Molar With Nerve Risk ### Scenario A 21-year-old has intermittent
pericoronitis around #32. Panoramic radiograph shows an impacted mandibular third molar with roots close to the inferior alveolar canal—darkening of roots and canal diversion signs. ### Clinical Reasoning This is
where your risk assessment becomes a patient safety tool. **Symptoms/Story:** Recurrent infection suggests a benefit to removal, but timing matters—don’t operate through severe acute infection if you can stabilize first.
**Anatomy/Imaging:** Panorex suggests proximity to the canal. This is a classic indication to consider **[EMPHASIS]CBCT** for 3D assessment. **Fitness:** Healthy, ASA I. **Execution Plan — Decision Point** You now have
choices: 1. Proceed with full extraction with informed consent about nerve risk. 2. Consider **[EMPHASIS]coronectomy** if roots are intimately associated with the canal and tooth is vital and appropriate. 3.
Refer to a specialist if risk is beyond your training/comfort. If CBCT confirms intimate contact, you might discuss coronectomy: - Remove crown, reduce to below bone level, avoid mobilizing roots.
- Explain that roots may migrate and rarely require later intervention. **Recovery Plan:** Pericoronitis patients need clear hygiene instructions, irrigation guidance, and follow-up. ### Communication Tip Instead of saying, “There’s
a risk of nerve damage,” quantify and contextualize: - “There is a small but real chance of temporary numbness, and a very small chance of permanent numbness. The scan helps
us choose the safest approach.” **Summary & Key Points** - CBCT can change your plan. - **[EMPHASIS]Inferior alveolar nerve risk** requires explicit consent and a tailored approach. - Coronectomy is
a risk-reduction strategy in selected cases. [PAUSE] --- ## 5) Case Study 3 — Acute Odontogenic Infection: When to Drain, When to Delay ### Scenario A 35-year-old presents with facial
swelling, trismus, and pain from a lower molar. Temperature is 38.3°C. The patient reports difficulty swallowing but can manage secretions. Intraoral exam: vestibular swelling, fluctuance. Tooth #19 is grossly carious.
### Priorities Infections can become life-threatening if they spread to deep spaces. Your first job is triage. **Red Flags** requiring urgent escalation: - Airway compromise signs: drooling, stridor, inability to
swallow saliva - Rapidly progressing swelling - Floor-of-mouth elevation, tongue displacement - Severe trismus preventing exam - Toxic appearance, dehydration In this case, mild dysphagia plus fever and trismus means
you must assess carefully. **Management Approach** - Evaluate airway and overall stability. - If localized vestibular abscess with fluctuance: **[EMPHASIS]incision and drainage** plus appropriate antibiotics. - Decide whether to extract
the source tooth now or delay until swelling is reduced—often extraction can be done at the same visit if access and anesthesia are adequate, but use judgment. **Antibiotics** Antibiotics are
adjunctive—drainage and source control are primary. - Choose based on local guidelines, allergies, and severity. - Provide clear duration and adherence instructions. **Follow-up** - Re-evaluate in 24–48 hours. - Give
strict return precautions: worsening swelling, fever, swallowing difficulty, breathing changes. **Summary & Key Points** - **[EMPHASIS]Airway assessment** comes first. - Drainage + source control beats antibiotics alone. - Early follow-up
prevents missed deterioration. [PAUSE] --- ## 6) Case Study 4 — Anticoagulated Patient Needing Extraction ### Scenario A 72-year-old needs extraction of #14 due to fracture. Medical history: atrial fibrillation
on apixaban; hypertension; prior stroke. The patient asks, “Should I stop my blood thinner?” ### Core Principle For many routine dental extractions, **continuing anticoagulation** with local hemostatic measures is safer
than stopping and risking thromboembolism. But you must individualize. **Steps** 1. Determine procedure invasiveness: single simple extraction versus multiple surgical extractions. 2. Review bleeding risk factors: liver disease, renal impairment,
concurrent antiplatelets, uncontrolled hypertension. 3. Coordinate with the prescribing physician when uncertainty exists. **In-Office Strategy** - Schedule early in the day and week. - Use minimally traumatic technique. - Consider
sutures and local hemostatics: oxidized cellulose, collagen plug, or topical tranexamic acid if available per protocol. - Provide firm gauze pressure instructions. **Consent Language** - “You may bleed a little
longer than average. Our plan is to control bleeding locally. The risk of stopping your medication may be more serious than the bleeding risk.” **Summary & Key Points** - Don’t
reflexively stop anticoagulants. - **[EMPHASIS]Local hemostasis** is your main tool. - Communicate and document medication decisions carefully. [PAUSE] --- ## 7) Case Study 5 — Post-Op Complication: Dry Socket vs
Infection vs Bleeding ### Scenario A: Pain 3 days after extraction A 28-year-old returns 72 hours after mandibular molar extraction with severe throbbing pain radiating to the ear. No swelling.
Bad taste. Socket looks partially empty with exposed bone. This strongly suggests **[EMPHASIS]alveolar osteitis**, or dry socket. **Management** - Irrigate gently to remove debris. - Place medicated dressing if your
protocol supports it. - Provide analgesia plan—often NSAIDs are helpful. - Avoid telling patients it’s “infected” if it’s not—language matters. ### Scenario B: Persistent bleeding the evening after extraction A
patient calls: gauze is soaked repeatedly. **Phone triage** - Confirm they are applying firm, continuous pressure with folded gauze for 20–30 minutes without checking every minute. - Ask about spitting,
rinsing, smoking—these dislodge clots. If uncontrolled: - Bring them in. - Identify source: socket ooze vs soft tissue tear. - Use suction sparingly. - Apply hemostatic agent and suture if
needed. - Consider systemic factors if unexpected. ### Scenario C: Swelling + fever 5 days after surgery This is more suggestive of infection or a developing abscess—requires exam, drainage if
indicated, and antibiotic consideration. **Summary & Key Points** - Day 2–4 severe pain without swelling often equals **[EMPHASIS]dry socket**. - Bleeding control starts with pressure and technique, then hemostatics/suturing. -
Swelling + fever later needs reassessment and possible drainage. [PAUSE] --- ## 8) Practical Habits That Improve Outcomes Before we wrap up, here are “small” behaviors that produce “big” results:
1. **Document clearly**: consent, imaging interpretation, meds, instructions, and follow-up plan. 2. Use **closed-loop instructions**: ask the patient to repeat key post-op steps back to you. 3. Prefer **risk-based imaging**:
don’t over-image, but don’t under-image when nerves or anatomy are uncertain. 4. Build a referral threshold: if a case exceeds your experience, referral is part of safe care—not failure. **Quick
Summary** - Use a structured approach like S-A-F-E-R. - Treat the patient, not just the tooth. - Anticipate complications and plan for recovery. [PAUSE] --- ## 9) Conclusion & Key
Takeaways Clinical oral surgery is the intersection of diagnosis, technical execution, and follow-up judgment. Case-based thinking trains you to anticipate complications before they happen and to communicate clearly when risk
is unavoidable. **Key takeaways from today:** - Apply **[EMPHASIS]S-A-F-E-R** to every case: symptoms, anatomy, fitness, execution, recovery. - Use imaging strategically—especially **[EMPHASIS]CBCT** when nerve proximity is suspected. - Infections require
triage: **[EMPHASIS]airway first**, then drainage and source control. - Anticoagulated patients often can be treated safely using **[EMPHASIS]local hemostasis**—avoid unnecessary interruption. - Recognize common post-op problems: **[EMPHASIS]dry socket**, bleeding, and
