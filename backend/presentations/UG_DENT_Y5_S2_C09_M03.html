Advanced questions often test *integration* more than recall. The patient doesn’t present as “renal” or “cardiac”—they present as physiology under stress. A reliable method is to anchor on three questions:
1. **What is the unstable problem?** Airway, breathing, circulation, mental status—then stabilize. 2. **What is the dominant physiologic derangement?** Think: oxygenation failure, perfusion failure, inflammation/sepsis, acid-base disturbance, endocrine collapse. 3.

Advanced questions test integration and prioritization. - Use a three-step framework: unstable problem → physiologic derangement → primary vs secondary organ effects. - When in doubt, reason from physiology. [PAUSE]
--- ## Section 2: Complex Topic Integration #1 — Shock + Acid-Base + Lactate (3:30–6:30) Let’s integrate shock states with acid-base patterns—classic exam territory. ### A. Shock Types in One
Minute - **Hypovolemic shock:** low preload → low cardiac output → cool, clammy; narrow pulse pressure. - **Cardiogenic shock:** pump failure → pulmonary edema, high JVP, cool extremities. - **Distributive
shock (sepsis, anaphylaxis):** low SVR → warm early, bounding pulses; later can become cool. - **Obstructive shock (PE, tamponade, tension pneumothorax):** impaired filling/outflow → JVP up; context clues matter. Now
layer acid-base: ### B. The Acid-Base Clues That “Talk Back” - **High anion gap metabolic acidosis** with high lactate suggests tissue hypoperfusion or mitochondrial dysfunction. - **Respiratory alkalosis** can be
an early sepsis clue (hyperventilation, anxiety, fever). - **Mixed disorders** are common in real life and on exams. [EMPHASIS]Lactate[/EMPHASIS] is not “a sepsis marker.” It’s a marker of physiologic stress—often
hypoperfusion, sometimes beta-agonists, seizures, liver dysfunction, or severe asthma. [PAUSE] ### Case Scenario 1: “The Confusing Lactate” A 62-year-old presents with fever, productive cough, RR 28, BP 86/52, HR 128.
Extremities are warm. Lactate is 4.8. ABG shows pH 7.48, PaCO₂ 30, HCO₃⁻ 22. Let’s translate: - Warm extremities + hypotension suggests distributive shock, likely sepsis. - ABG: alkalemic with
low CO₂—this is primarily [EMPHASIS]respiratory alkalosis[/EMPHASIS], common early in sepsis. - Lactate high supports high physiologic stress and possible hypoperfusion. **What’s the best next step?** On most exams: early sepsis
bundle principles—**IV fluids**, **blood cultures**, **broad-spectrum antibiotics**, and **vasopressors** if fluid-refractory. High-yield nuance: Do not wait for hypotension to give antibiotics. And don’t be distracted by alkalosis—sepsis can start with
respiratory alkalosis and evolve into metabolic acidosis later. [PAUSE] ### Mini-Summary: Key Points - Shock type + acid-base pattern is a powerful diagnostic shortcut. - Early sepsis often shows respiratory
