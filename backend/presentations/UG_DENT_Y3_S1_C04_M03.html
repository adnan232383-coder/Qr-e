--- ## 2) The “Integration Mindset”: How to Think Like a Prosthodontist In Prosthodontics, the hardest cases—and the hardest exam questions—usually involve competing priorities. You’ll often hear: “What’s the best
next step?” The trick is that “best” depends on what’s *most limiting*. A useful hierarchy is: 1. **Medical and behavioral factors**: Can the patient tolerate treatment? Is xerostomia, bruxism, or
poor attendance a limiting factor? 2. **Disease control**: Active caries, uncontrolled periodontal disease, unresolved endodontic pathology. 3. **Prognosis of abutments**: Especially periodontal support, crown-root ratio, ferrule, and restorability. 4. **Occlusion
and function**: Parafunction, vertical dimension, anterior guidance, interferences. 5. **Esthetics and patient expectations**. 6. **Prosthesis design**: materials, connectors, clasping, pontic design, etc. Think of this as “stability first, then design.”
**Key point:** If disease is active, *prosth is premature*. Many exam questions test whether you’ll jump to impressions and crowns before stabilizing the foundation. [PAUSE] **Quick summary:** - Start with
medical/behavioral risk → then disease control → then evaluate abutments → then occlusion → then design. - Exam questions often reward the *sequence* more than the final prosthesis choice. [PAUSE]
--- ## 3) Fixed Prosthodontics: When Crowns and Bridges Become “Advanced” ### 3A) Retention and Resistance: The Geometry Still Rules For full-coverage restorations and FPDs, the classic exam theme is:
why will this fail? Retention and resistance depend on: - **Preparation height** - **Total occlusal convergence (TOC)** - **Surface area** - **Auxiliary features** like grooves/boxes - **Cement selection**, but cement
cannot compensate for poor geometry forever If you have **short clinical crowns**, the “advanced” move isn’t fancy cement—it’s improving [EMPHASIS]resistance form[/EMPHASIS] with grooves, reducing TOC, or considering crown lengthening—assuming biologic
width and esthetics permit. [PAUSE] ### Clinical Example 1: Short Clinical Crown in a Bruxer Imagine a 52-year-old patient with severe attrition and fractured restorations. The mandibular molar has only
2.5 mm of axial wall height after caries removal. They want a crown. Your integrated plan: 1. Identify bruxism risk: high occlusal load. 2. Improve resistance form: add **proximal grooves**,
minimize TOC. 3. Consider **crown lengthening** if periodontal parameters allow and the patient accepts. 4. Material choice: a strong restorative material may help, but cannot fix a non-retentive prep. 5.
Provide a **night guard** after delivery. **Exam angle:** If asked “most important factor to prevent dislodgement,” the answer is usually preparation geometry—*not* stronger cement. [PAUSE] ### 3B) Ferrule and Restorability:
The Endo-Prosth Intersection Another classic integration point: endodontically treated teeth. A tooth with a post and core still needs [EMPHASIS]ferrule[/EMPHASIS]—ideally 1.5–2.0 mm of sound tooth structure circumferentially. Without it, fracture
risk goes up, especially under lateral forces. **Clinical decision point:** - If ferrule is inadequate, consider crown lengthening or orthodontic extrusion *before* definitive crown. - If the tooth cannot be
made restorable, extraction and replacement may be more predictable. [PAUSE] ### 3C) Bridge (FPD) Design: Abutment Selection and Biomechanics A fixed partial denture is only as good as its abutments.
Review the big risks: - **Long-span FPDs** increase flexure and stress. - **Pier abutments** can create a fulcrum effect. - **Cantilevers** amplify force—especially in posterior regions. **Pearl:** When a pier
abutment exists, a [EMPHASIS]non-rigid connector[/EMPHASIS] is often indicated to reduce stress concentration—commonly placed on the *distal* of the pier abutment (depending on the case and movement pattern), allowing independent movement
segments. [PAUSE] **Section summary — Fixed Prosthodontics Key Points:** - Geometry drives retention/resistance. - Ferrule is critical for endo-treated teeth. - Span length, pier abutments, and cantilevers change stress distribution—design
matters. [PAUSE] --- ## 4) Removable Partial Dentures: “Advanced” Means Design Logic RPD questions become easier when you always ask: **Where is support coming from? Where will it rotate? How
do I control that rotation?** ### 4A) Support, Stability, and Indirect Retention For distal extension RPDs, the denture base is supported by: - The **abutment teeth** (through rests) - The
**residual ridge** (compressible tissue) This difference causes rotation around a [EMPHASIS]fulcrum line[/EMPHASIS]. That’s why **indirect retainers** matter: they resist rotational displacement away from tissue. If an exam asks, “What’s the
purpose of an indirect retainer?” the best phrasing is: **to resist lifting of the distal extension base away from the ridge by counteracting rotation around the fulcrum line.** [PAUSE] ###
4B) Stress-Release and Clasp Selection Clasp design depends on whether the case is tooth-supported or tooth-tissue supported. - For **tooth-supported** (bounded edentulous spaces): more rigid designs may be acceptable. -
For **distal extension**: use designs that reduce torque on abutments, like an [EMPHASIS]RPI system[/EMPHASIS] (Rest, Proximal plate, I-bar) when indicated. Also remember soft-tissue undercuts, vestibular depth, and esthetic zones can
contraindicate certain clasps. [PAUSE] ### Clinical Example 2: Kennedy Class I with Weak Abutments A 68-year-old patient is missing bilateral mandibular molars: Kennedy Class I. The remaining premolars show mild
mobility but are maintainable with perio therapy. The patient has a moderate gag reflex and wants something “stable.” Best thinking: 1. This is **distal extension** → expect rotation. 2. Plan
**mesial rests** on terminal abutments to move the fulcrum line favorably and reduce distal tipping. 3. Use an **RPI**-type approach if anatomy allows. 4. Place an **indirect retainer** anteriorly—often a
rest on a canine or premolar—to resist lifting. 5. Emphasize **functional impression concept** or altered cast technique in mandibular distal extension cases to improve base adaptation and reduce abutment torque.
**Exam angle:** If asked “most critical factor for minimizing torque,” the answer is often *design choices* (mesial rest, stress-releasing clasp) and accurate support from the base. [PAUSE] **Section summary —
RPD Key Points:** - Distal extension cases rotate—design around the fulcrum line. - Indirect retention is about resisting rotation away from tissue. - Mesial rests and stress-release clasp designs reduce
harmful torque. [PAUSE] --- ## 5) Occlusion and Prosthodontics: Where Cases Get Tricky Occlusion shows up everywhere—in crown fractures, denture instability, TMD symptoms, and patient dissatisfaction. ### 5A) Three High-Yield
Occlusal Ideas 1. **Force direction matters:** Vertical forces are better tolerated than lateral forces. 2. [EMPHASIS]Anterior guidance[/EMPHASIS] and canine guidance can reduce posterior lateral loading—when appropriate. 3. The prosthesis should
harmonize with the patient’s functional patterns, not fight them. In complete denture concepts, balanced occlusion may be emphasized for stability during excursions; in fixed cases, you might favor mutually protected
occlusion—depending on context. [PAUSE] ### Clinical Example 3: “My Crown Keeps Fracturing” A patient reports repeated fracture of a maxillary first molar crown—twice in three years. Your diagnostic checklist: -
Look for **parafunction** (wear facets, linea alba, masseter hypertrophy). - Evaluate **occlusal scheme**: is there a working or non-working interference? - Check opposing dentition: natural enamel, ceramic, implant restoration? -
Assess material thickness and preparation reduction. Management: - Adjust occlusion to eliminate interferences. - Consider stronger material selection and ensure adequate reduction. - Provide a night guard if bruxism is
likely. - If the tooth is endodontically treated, verify ferrule and core integrity. **Exam angle:** recurring fracture is rarely “bad luck.” It’s typically [EMPHASIS]occlusal overload[/EMPHASIS] or inadequate material thickness—often both.
[PAUSE] **Occlusion mini-summary:** - Lateral forces are destructive—manage guidance and interferences. - Fractures are usually biomechanical: thickness, design, overload. - A night guard is a prosthodontic “insurance policy” in bruxers.
[PAUSE] --- ## 6) Common Failure Patterns and How Exams Test Them Let’s connect failures to causes—because exam questions often present the failure and ask for the underlying reason. ###
6A) Debonded Crowns Common causes: - Short axial walls or excessive TOC - Contaminated bonding field - Inadequate resistance form - Heavy lateral occlusion Best prevention: - Improve prep geometry
and add features - Control moisture and isolation - Check occlusion carefully ### 6B) Recurrent Caries at Margins Common causes: - Poor marginal adaptation - Overhanging margins - High caries
risk patient without fluoride strategy Prevention: - Smooth, well-adapted margins - Caries risk management: fluoride, diet counseling, recall ### 6C) RPD Soreness and Instability Common causes: - Poor support/extension -
Occlusal discrepancies - Overextended borders - Poor altered cast management in distal extension Prevention: - Proper impression technique and extension - Correct occlusion and adjustment protocols [PAUSE] **Key point:** Exams
love “most likely cause” questions. Tie the complication to a foundational principle: geometry, support, occlusion, or disease control. [PAUSE] --- ## 7) Exam Strategy: A Simple Framework for Case Questions
When you get a long stem with radiographs and findings, use this sequence: 1. **What is the chief complaint?** (function, esthetics, pain, looseness) 2. **What is the diagnosis and risk
profile?** (caries risk, perio status, bruxism) 3. **What is the prognosis of key teeth?** 4. **What must be done first?** (disease control, endo, perio, extractions) 5. **Which prosthesis fits the
constraints?** fixed vs removable; design features; maintenance 6. **What will make it fail?** and how do you prevent that? If the question asks “best next step,” it is frequently step
4—not step 5. [PAUSE] **Rapid review checkpoint (say it with me):** - Stabilize disease. - Respect biomechanics. - Design to control forces and movement. - Plan for maintenance. [PAUSE] ---
