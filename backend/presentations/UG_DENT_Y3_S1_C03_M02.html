Clinical Decision-Making

Now the clinical question becomes: **What is appropriate treatment today?** Options include:

Vital pulp therapy (case dependent and increasingly evidence-supported in select situations)

Root canal therapy

Extraction (if non-restorable)

Percussion: variable, may be mildly positive

Bite test: sharp pain when biting on a cusp, sometimes pain on release

Differential Diagnosis

This is where many clinicians get trapped. The symptoms point strongly toward:

[EMPHASIS]Cracked tooth syndrome[/EMPHASIS]

High occlusion from new restoration

Early apical inflammation (less likely if cold is normal and radiographs are clean)

Clinical Decision-Making

Your next step is not automatically endodontic treatment. Instead:

1. Evaluate occlusion and adjust if indicated.

2. Remove restoration if needed to visualize and test.

3. Consider interim stabilization: banding or a provisional crown to splint cusps.

Section 5: Clinical Scenario — The Emergency Endodontic Patient

Emergency care is a major part of clinical endodontics. Here are two common emergency patterns:

Pattern A: Severe Spontaneous Pain, No Swelling

Often [EMPHASIS]symptomatic irreversible pulpitis[/EMPHASIS].

Practical emergency options include:

Pulpotomy/pulpectomy to reduce pain

Initial canal debridement and irrigation

Pattern B: Swelling and Systemic Involvement

If you see diffuse swelling, fever, malaise, trismus, or airway concerns, step back. These are red flags. Management may

Establish drainage when possible

Appropriate antibiotics *when indicated*

Urgent referral if there are signs of spreading infection or airway compromise

Key Points

Emergency endodontics is about prioritizing: pain control, infection control, and safety.

Always identify red flags that require urgent escalation.

Section 7: Putting It Into Practice — A Chairside Decision Checklist

When you’re under time pressure in clinic, use a quick checklist:

1. **Restorability:** Is the tooth restorable after endodontics?

2. **Periodontal status:** Any vertical defects, isolated deep probing, mobility?

3. **Pulp tests:** Vital vs necrotic—does it fit the story?

Key Points

Don’t treat a radiograph—treat the patient.

Don’t treat the pulp alone—consider the whole tooth prognosis.

A structured checklist reduces errors and improves consistency.
