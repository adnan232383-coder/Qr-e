includes: - **Inflammatory conditions** like aphthous ulcers or oral lichen planus - **Infections** such as candidiasis or herpes - **Reactive lesions** like fibromas from chronic irritation - **Autoimmune and systemic

in dentistry can be life-changing. - Your role includes recognizing normal vs abnormal and knowing the next step. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 2: Key Terms You Must Know (2:10–5:00) Let’s

Principle 1: Start With a Systematic Description Before labeling anything, describe it. Use a consistent checklist: 1. **Location**: tongue, floor of mouth, palate, gingiva, buccal mucosa 2. **Size**: measure in
millimeters or centimeters 3. **Shape and border**: regular vs irregular, well-defined vs diffuse 4. **Color**: white, red, pigmented, mixed 5. **Surface texture**: smooth, verrucous, ulcerated 6. **Consistency** (if palpable): soft,

That description becomes your clinical “data,” and it protects you legally and clinically. [PAUSE] ### Principle 2: Use Pattern Recognition—But Don’t Trust It Blindly Certain patterns strongly suggest certain categories:
- **White wipeable plaques** → often fungal or debris-related - **Grouped vesicles → ulcers** → often viral - **Ulcer with indurated border** → consider malignancy - **Bilateral, symmetric striations** →
consider lichen planus But pattern recognition is a starting point, not a conclusion. Always integrate risk factors and persistence. [PAUSE] ### Principle 3: Time Matters—The “Two-Week Rule” A practical rule
in oral mucosal pathology: If a lesion has no clear cause and persists beyond **two weeks**, it deserves re-evaluation and often referral or biopsy. This is especially important for ulcers
and red/white patches. [PAUSE] ### Principle 4: Red Flags—Know What You Cannot Miss Red flags include: - Persistent ulcer > 2 weeks - [EMPHASIS]Induration (firmness) on palpation - Unexplained bleeding
- Mixed red and white lesion (erythroleukoplakia) - Non-wipeable red patch ([EMPHASIS]erythroplakia) - Numbness, dysphagia, voice change - Neck mass or lymphadenopathy - Lesion on high-risk sites: lateral/ventral tongue, floor
of mouth, soft palate complex High-risk doesn’t mean diagnosis—high-risk means *act promptly*. [PAUSE] ### Principle 5: Risk Assessment Is Part of the Exam Ask targeted questions: - Tobacco use (smoked
or smokeless) - Alcohol consumption - HPV risk factors (context-dependent) - Immunosuppression (HIV, transplant meds, chemo) - Diabetes and xerostomia risks - Trauma (sharp tooth, biting, dentures) - New medications
(drug reactions can mimic disease) [PAUSE] **Section Summary** - Describe first, label second. - Use patterns, but confirm with time course and risk. - Apply the two-week rule. - Identify
