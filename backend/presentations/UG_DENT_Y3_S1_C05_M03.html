3) Section Two: Deep Caries and Pulp Protection — What’s the Goal?

Deep caries cases are where students often over-treat—or under-protect.

Preserve pulp vitality when possible

Achieve a durable seal

Avoid postoperative sensitivity

Concept: Selective Caries Removal

In deep lesions near the pulp, modern operative dentistry supports **[EMPHASIS] selective caries removal to soft dentin*

Why? Because the biggest predictor of pulpal healing is **a long-term seal**, not aggressive excavation.

Liners and Bases — A Practical Rule Set

**Liner**: thin layer, biologic protection (e.g., calcium silicate materials, calcium hydroxide in some protocols).

**Base**: thicker, replaces dentin, provides support (e.g., [EMPHASIS] RMGI in many deep cases).

A common practical approach:

If you have a pinpoint near exposure or very deep area: consider a **bioceramic liner** or calcium hydroxide *only at the deepest spot*.

Case Scenario 1: Deep Occlusal Caries on Mandibular First Molar

A 19-year-old patient has deep occlusal caries on #30. Cold test is normal, no spontaneous pain, no percussion sensitivi

**What’s the best approach?**

Diagnosis: deep caries, vital pulp, reversible pulpitis likely.

Plan: rubber dam isolation. Remove peripheral caries to firm dentin; selectively remove deep caries to avoid exposure.

Quick Summary

[EMPHASIS] Seal matters.

Selective caries removal is often appropriate in deep, vital cases.

Avoid unnecessary interfaces.

4) Section Three: Adhesion Strategy — Choose, Don’t Guess

Adhesion questions show up everywhere—post-op sensitivity, marginal staining, debonding, secondary caries.

Enamel vs Dentin — Different Worlds

**Enamel** bonding benefits strongly from **[EMPHASIS] phosphoric acid etching** and a resin infiltrated micro-porosity.

**Dentin** bonding is more complex: fluid, collagen, smear layer, tubules.

Adhesive Systems (High-Yield Review)

Most curricula emphasize:

**Etch-and-rinse (total-etch)**: great enamel bond, technique-sensitive dentin.

**Self-etch**: less sensitivity risk, may be weaker on enamel if not selectively etched.

**Universal adhesives**: flexible; can be used in self-etch or etch-and-rinse modes.

When isolation is compromised—subgingival margins, heavy bleeding, salivary contamination—your plan should adapt:

Improve isolation: retraction cord, hemostatic agents, rubber dam clamps, isolation systems.

Consider margin elevation strategies if acceptable in your program.

Case Scenario 2: Class V Lesion with Cervical Margin on Dentin/Cementum

A 54-year-old has a non-carious cervical lesion with gingival margin on cementum, mild bleeding on probing, high plaque

First: control bleeding and plaque—field management is part of the procedure.

Use rubber dam or alternative isolation; consider retraction.

Adhesive strategy: selective enamel etch if enamel present; dentin bonding with a compatible adhesive; consider a material with good marginal adaptation at dentin margins.

Key Point Summary

[EMPHASIS] Selective enamel etch + universal adhesive is a common exam-safe approach.

Isolation is often the real deciding factor.

Dentin margins and bleeding should change your plan.

Principles for Class II Composite Success

Stable matrix with proper contour (sectional matrix systems are commonly preferred)

Firm separation (ring system)

Tight adaptation at the gingival margin

Incremental build-up that recreates the marginal ridge and contact

Case Scenario 3: Deep Class II Box with Gingival Margin on Dentin

A 32-year-old has a DO lesion on #19. Gingival margin is 1 mm below CEJ on dentin. You can isolate with a rubber dam.

**What matters most?**

Matrix and seal at the gingival margin

Adhesive selection suitable for dentin margin

Quick Summary

Most Class II failures are **contact and marginal seal** failures.

Use a matrix system that can predictably create a tight contact.

Cure well—especially at deep boxes.

Postoperative Sensitivity — High-Yield Differential

When a patient returns after a new composite with sensitivity, think systematically:

1. **High occlusion** (most common and easiest to fix)

2. Marginal leakage / bonding failure

3. Exposed dentin due to inadequate sealing

Key Point Summary

[EMPHASIS] High occlusion is the first thing to check in post-op sensitivity.

Shrinkage stress is real—technique matters.

7) Section Six: Cracked Tooth, Cuspal Coverage, and When Direct Becomes Indirect

Not every tooth should get a direct composite.

Recognizing When Cuspal Coverage Is Indicated

Consider cuspal coverage when:

There’s a crack undermining a cusp

A large MOD exists with thin cusps

There’s heavy occlusion or parafunction

Provisional stabilization or immediate cuspal coverage to splint the tooth

Evaluate occlusion

Quick Summary

Big restorations change tooth biomechanics.

Cracks often need stabilization and coverage, not just a new filling.

8) Section Seven: Exam Prep — How to Approach Multi-Step Questions

Here’s a reliable method for complex exam questions:

1. **Identify the diagnosis** (caries, crack, defective restoration, pulp status).

2. **Locate the margin** (enamel vs dentin/cementum; supragingival vs subgingival).

3. **Assess isolation** (rubber dam feasible? bleeding?).

Rapid-Fire High-Yield Reminders

When in doubt: **control the field** first.

Deep lesion + vital asymptomatic pulp: prioritize **seal and pulp preservation**.

Dentin/cementum margins + moisture challenge: consider **RMGI** or strategies to improve isolation.

Post-op sensitivity: check **occlusion** first.
