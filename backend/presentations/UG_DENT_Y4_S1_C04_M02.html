## Section 1: A Practical Framework for Oral Medicine Cases (0:45–3:00) Before we dive into cases, let’s set a simple framework you can use repeatedly—whether the complaint is an ulcer,
a white patch, or burning mouth symptoms. ### Step 1: Start with the chief complaint—then translate it Patients often say: “I have a sore,” “a patch,” “a bump,” or “my
mouth burns.” Your job is to translate that into a clinical problem: - Ulceration? - White lesion? - Pigmentation? - Swelling? - Pain without visible findings? ### Step 2: Take
a targeted history (the “OPQRST” plus risk) Use symptom analysis: - **Onset**: sudden or gradual? - **Provocation/Palliation**: worse with spicy foods, trauma, stress? - **Quality**: burning, sharp, throbbing? - **Region/Radiation**:
localized or diffuse? - **Severity**: impact on eating/speaking? - **Timing**: duration and pattern (intermittent vs persistent)? Then add key risk questions: - Tobacco (smoked/smokeless), alcohol, betel quid - Immunosuppression, diabetes,
steroid inhaler use - Sexual history when relevant (HPV, syphilis risk) - Medications (e.g., NSAIDs, nicorandil, bisphosphonates) - Recent dental treatment or trauma ### Step 3: Perform a structured exam
- Extraoral: lymph nodes, skin lesions, lip changes - Intraoral: site, size, color, texture, margins, induration, tenderness - **Palpation matters**: [EMPHASIS] induration[/EMPHASIS] and fixation raise concern. ### Step 4: Decide:
reassure, treat, investigate, or refer A useful mental rule: - If it’s not improving in **2 weeks**, or has red flags, escalate. [PAUSE] **Quick Summary** - A consistent framework prevents
missed diagnoses. - History + palpation + risk assessment drive decisions. - The 2-week rule helps you catch serious disease early. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 2: Case Study 1 —
The Persistent Ulcer (3:00–5:30) ### Clinical Scenario A 58-year-old man presents with “a sore on my tongue” for 3 weeks. It’s mildly painful. He smokes one pack per day and
drinks alcohol daily. No recent dental work. On exam: a 1.2 cm ulcer on the lateral border of the tongue. The margins appear slightly raised. On palpation, the base feels
firm. [PAUSE] ### Clinical Reasoning Let’s build a differential diagnosis for an oral ulcer: - Traumatic ulcer - Aphthous ulcer (minor/major) - Erosive lichen planus - Infection (HSV—usually multiple; syphilis—consider
risk) - Medication-related ulceration - [EMPHASIS] oral squamous cell carcinoma[/EMPHASIS] (OSCC) Now, key discriminators: - **Duration > 2 weeks**: not typical for minor trauma or minor aphthae. - **Location**: lateral
tongue is a common OSCC site. - **Risk factors**: tobacco and alcohol. - **Induration**: this is a major red flag. ### Management Plan This is not a “watch and wait”
case. Your next steps: 1. Explain concern in clear, calm terms: “Most ulcers heal quickly, but because this has lasted 3 weeks and feels firm, we need to investigate promptly.”
2. Arrange an **urgent referral** for specialist evaluation and biopsy. 3. Document lesion description and include clinical photos if possible. 4. Check for cervical lymphadenopathy and document findings. ### Teaching
Point If you remember one rule from ulcer cases: A persistent ulcer with [EMPHASIS] induration[/EMPHASIS] requires biopsy until proven otherwise. [PAUSE] **Key Points** - Persistent ulcer + risk factors +
induration = urgent referral. - Don’t mask symptoms with repeated topical steroids before diagnosis. - Document carefully: size, site, duration, palpation findings. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 3: Case Study 2

Exam (8:00–10:05) ### Clinical Scenario A 63-year-old woman reports a burning sensation on the tongue and palate for 6 months. It worsens through the day. She says food tastes “different,”
and her mouth feels dry. She’s frustrated because “everyone tells me it looks normal.” On exam: oral mucosa appears healthy. No ulceration, no obvious erythema, no removable plaques. [PAUSE] ###
Clinical Reasoning This scenario raises the possibility of [EMPHASIS] Burning Mouth Syndrome[/EMPHASIS] (BMS), but only after excluding secondary causes. Think in two buckets: #### 1) Secondary burning (find the cause)
Common secondary causes include: - Xerostomia (medications, Sjögren’s) - Candidiasis (may be subtle) - Nutritional deficiencies (iron, B12, folate) - Diabetes - Thyroid disease - Contact allergies or irritants -
Reflux - Parafunctional habits (tongue thrusting, bruxism) #### 2) Primary BMS (neuropathic pain) Typical features: - Normal clinical appearance - Burning increases during the day - May include dysgeusia and
subjective dry mouth ### Workup and Management 1. Validate the symptom experience: “Your exam looks normal, but your pain is real—and we can work through likely causes.” 2. Screen for
red flags: weight loss, dysphagia, focal mass—if present, escalate. 3. Basic investigations: CBC, ferritin/iron, B12/folate, glucose/HbA1c, thyroid as indicated. 4. If secondary causes ruled out: - Discuss neuropathic pain framework
- Consider topical options (e.g., saliva substitutes if dry; sometimes topical clonazepam is used under specialist guidance) - Address anxiety, sleep, and coping strategies - Referral to oral medicine/orofacial pain
specialist when persistent [PAUSE] **Key Points** - BMS is a diagnosis of exclusion. - Validate symptoms to reduce distress and improve adherence. - Treat causes you can find; manage neuropathic
pain thoughtfully if primary BMS. [PAUSE] --- ## Section 5: Case Study 4 — Swelling and the “Do Not Miss” Rule (10:05–11:20) ### Clinical Scenario A 29-year-old presents with a
