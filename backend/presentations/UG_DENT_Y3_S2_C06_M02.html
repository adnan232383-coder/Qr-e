## Pediatric Dentistry I — Module: Clinical Applications ### Video Lecture Script (≈12 minutes / ~1800 words) --- ## 1) Introduction & Learning Objectives Welcome to **Pediatric Dentistry I: Clinical
Applications**. In this module, we move from concepts to **real-world practice**—the day-to-day clinical decisions you’ll make when a child is in the chair, a caregiver is anxious, and time is
limited. Pediatric dentistry is rarely “one problem, one solution.” Instead, it’s a balance of diagnosis, prevention, behavior guidance, restorative choices, and risk management—while keeping the child safe and building trust.
By the end of this lecture, you should be able to: 1. Apply a structured approach to pediatric clinical decision-making using **caries risk**, **behavior**, and **medical status**. 2. Recognize and
manage common clinical scenarios in pediatric dentistry. 3. Choose appropriate preventive and restorative strategies for primary teeth and early mixed dentition. 4. Communicate treatment options clearly to caregivers and document
decisions appropriately. [PAUSE] --- ## 2) A Practical Framework for Pediatric Clinical Decision-Making Before we jump into cases, let’s establish a simple clinical framework you can use in almost every
pediatric appointment. Think of it as five steps: ### Step 1: Safety first — Medical status and consent Confirm medical history, medications, allergies, and any red flags. Ensure informed consent,
and verify who can legally consent for the child. ### Step 2: Behavior and communication plan Assess cooperation level and plan behavior guidance accordingly—tell-show-do, positive reinforcement, distraction, protective stabilization only
when indicated and permitted, or referral for sedation/general anesthesia when necessary. ### Step 3: Disease level — Caries risk and activity Determine [EMPHASIS]caries risk[/EMPHASIS] (low, moderate, high) and whether lesions
are active. Use risk factors like frequent sugar exposure, plaque, enamel defects, prior caries, and caregiver caries history. ### Step 4: Tooth-level diagnosis For each tooth: Is the lesion non-cavitated
or cavitated? Is it cleansable? Is it in enamel or dentin? Is there pulpal involvement? Radiographs when indicated. ### Step 5: Choose the least invasive effective plan Start with prevention
and non-invasive options when possible, then minimally invasive restorative care, then full coverage or pulp therapy when needed. **Key point:** In pediatrics, the “best” treatment is the one that is
clinically sound **and** feasible for that child today. [PAUSE] **Quick Summary:** - Start with safety and behavior, then risk and diagnosis, then treatment selection. - Aim for **least invasive** and
**most durable** based on child and family factors. [PAUSE] --- ## 3) Case Study 1 — Early Childhood Caries in a Preschooler ### Scenario A 3-year-old presents with the caregiver
reporting “brown spots” on the upper front teeth. The child is shy and cries during examination. Diet history reveals frequent snacking and bedtime milk in a bottle. No significant medical
history. ### Clinical findings You observe white spot lesions and brown cavitations on maxillary primary incisors. Plaque is heavy. The caregiver reports brushing “sometimes,” usually without fluoride toothpaste. ### Step-by-step
clinical reasoning **1) Caries risk:** This is [EMPHASIS]high caries risk[/EMPHASIS]. Risk factors include frequent sugar exposure, poor plaque control, and multiple active lesions. **2) Behavior:** Limited cooperation. You need a
staged plan. **3) Diagnostic essentials:** - Visual-tactile assessment with good lighting and drying. - Consider bitewings if cooperation permits—but at age 3, it may be challenging. If radiographs aren’t possible
today, plan to attempt again soon once rapport improves. ### Treatment planning discussion Here’s a practical approach: **A) Immediate disease control (today):** - Caregiver counseling: eliminate bedtime bottle with milk/juice;
transition to water only at night. - Brushing twice daily with **smear** of fluoride toothpaste. - Apply [EMPHASIS]fluoride varnish[/EMPHASIS] in office. - Consider [EMPHASIS]silver diamine fluoride[/EMPHASIS] (SDF) to arrest cavitated
lesions if restorations aren’t feasible immediately. Explain staining clearly. **B) Restorative plan (next visits):** - If anterior teeth have significant cavitation: options include glass ionomer interim restorations, strip crowns, or—if
extensive—referral for treatment under sedation/GA. - Decision depends on extent, esthetic needs, cooperation, and caregiver goals. ### Communication example (caregiver-friendly) “Your child has active cavities that are progressing. Our first
goal is to stop them from getting worse. Today we’ll place a strong fluoride coating and talk about home changes. If your child can’t tolerate fillings right now, we can
use a medicine called SDF that helps stop cavities, but it turns the decayed areas dark. Then we’ll plan restorations when your child is more comfortable—or consider treatment under deeper
sedation if needed.” [PAUSE] **Key points from Case 1:** - Early childhood caries requires **disease control + behavior planning**, not just fillings. - Use fluoride varnish and consider SDF as
a practical interim measure. - Be explicit about home care and feeding habits—especially nighttime exposures. [PAUSE] --- ## 4) Case Study 2 — Deep Caries in a Primary Molar: “Toothache
After Cold” ### Scenario A 6-year-old in early mixed dentition presents with “pain when drinking cold” in the lower left. The child is cooperative. Medical history is noncontributory. ### Clinical
findings Tooth: mandibular second primary molar has a large occlusal carious lesion. Bitewing shows caries into dentin approaching the pulp but no furcation radiolucency. No swelling. Percussion is normal. Pain
is brief and stimulus-related. ### Clinical reasoning This sounds like [EMPHASIS]reversible pulpitis[/EMPHASIS] or deep caries without frank pulpal necrosis. ### Clinical decision: Restore vs pulp therapy? In primary teeth, we
decide based on symptoms, caries extent, and pulp status. **If there is no spontaneous pain, no swelling, no pathologic mobility, and no radiographic signs of infection:** - Consider indirect pulp
treatment (IPT) or selective caries removal with a well-sealed restoration. **Restoration choice:** For a primary molar with extensive multi-surface caries, a [EMPHASIS]stainless steel crown[/EMPHASIS] (SSC) is often the most durable
option. ### Practical plan 1. Local anesthesia and isolation (rubber dam if possible). 2. Caries removal strategy: selective removal near the pulp to avoid exposure if indicated. 3. Place a
suitable liner/base if needed and restore with SSC. 4. Post-op instructions and follow-up. ### “What if” variation If the child had spontaneous nighttime pain, swelling, or a furcation radiolucency—then think
[EMPHASIS]irreversible pulpitis[/EMPHASIS] or necrosis and consider pulpotomy/pulpectomy or extraction depending on restorability and exfoliation timeline. [PAUSE] **Key points from Case 2:** - Symptoms and radiographs guide pulp decisions. - For
big primary molar lesions, SSCs often outperform large multi-surface fillings. - A well-sealed restoration is central to long-term success. [PAUSE] --- ## 5) Case Study 3 — Trauma: Avulsed Permanent
Incisor on the Playground ### Scenario An 8-year-old arrives 20 minutes after a fall. Caregiver says the front tooth “came out.” The tooth is brought in a napkin. Child is
upset but alert. No loss of consciousness, but lip is bleeding. ### First priority: Rule out medical emergency Assess for head injury red flags and refer to emergency care if
needed. Manage soft tissue injuries and evaluate tetanus status when indicated. ### Critical clinical concept At age 8, the maxillary central incisor is a [EMPHASIS]permanent tooth[/EMPHASIS]. An avulsed permanent tooth
is a time-sensitive emergency. ### Best practice approach (high-level) - Handle the tooth by the crown only. - If dirty, gently rinse briefly with saline. Avoid scraping the root. -
Ideally replant immediately if conditions allow and the child is cooperative. - If immediate replantation isn’t feasible, store in an appropriate medium (milk, saline, or specialized solution). A dry napkin
is not ideal. ### In-office management - Local anesthesia if needed, gentle irrigation of socket, replant tooth, confirm position. - Splint with a flexible splint as indicated. - Prescribe antibiotics
per guidelines and consider chlorhexidine rinses if age appropriate. - Refer for endodontic follow-up; immature teeth may need regenerative considerations. ### Communication emphasis Explain urgency and follow-up needs: “Today is
about getting the tooth stabilized. The next phase is monitoring and possibly nerve treatment depending on root development.” [PAUSE] **Key points from Case 3:** - Avulsed permanent teeth are **time
critical**. - Storage medium matters; dry time worsens prognosis. - Stabilization today, endodontic planning and long-term monitoring later. [PAUSE] --- ## 6) Case Study 4 — The Anxious Child and
the “Failed” Appointment ### Scenario A 5-year-old needs restoration of a primary molar. At the first appointment, the child cries, refuses to open, and kicks. The caregiver says, “Just hold
him down and do it.” ### Clinical reality This is a clinical scenario where your decision-making must include ethics, safety, and long-term cooperation. ### Structured approach **1) Reassess the urgency**
Is there pain, swelling, systemic involvement? If not urgent, you have time to build cooperation. **2) Choose behavior guidance intentionally** Start with [EMPHASIS]tell-show-do[/EMPHASIS], voice control where appropriate, positive reinforcement, and
short visits. Consider desensitization: exam-only visit, then fluoride varnish, then simple procedure. **3) Consider pharmacologic options appropriately** If the child has extensive needs or cannot cooperate despite appropriate nonpharmacologic techniques,
consider nitrous oxide or refer for sedation/GA depending on case complexity, health status, and resources. **4) Protective stabilization** This is not a “default.” It must follow local regulations, informed consent,
and be used only when necessary for safety and urgent care, with documentation. ### Sample caregiver explanation “Our goal is safe, quality dentistry while helping your child learn that the
dental office can be okay. Forcing treatment can create long-term fear and may not be safe. We can take a stepwise approach—short visits to build trust—or use nitrous oxide, or
in some cases complete care under deeper sedation.” [PAUSE] **Key points from Case 4:** - Behavior guidance is a clinical skill, not an afterthought. - Avoid escalating force when it’s
not urgent—plan staged care. - Document consent, rationale, and the chosen guidance method. [PAUSE] --- ## 7) Putting It All Together: Chairside “Clinical Scenario Drill” Let’s do a rapid drill
using three questions you can ask yourself in any pediatric case: 1. **Is it urgent?** Pain, infection, trauma, swelling, systemic signs—these push you toward same-day intervention. 2. **Can I do
