trap: equating statistical significance with clinical importance. ### A) Effect Size and Clinical Relevance When you see a result like “p < 0.05,” your next question should be: **How big
is the effect?** In dentistry, a small statistically significant improvement may not matter clinically—especially if it adds cost, chair time, or risk. Key effect measures include: - [EMPHASIS]Risk Ratio (RR)[/EMPHASIS]
and [EMPHASIS]Odds Ratio (OR)[/EMPHASIS] for binary outcomes - [EMPHASIS]Mean Difference[/EMPHASIS] or [EMPHASIS]Standardized Mean Difference[/EMPHASIS] for continuous outcomes - [EMPHASIS]Number Needed to Treat (NNT)[/EMPHASIS] to make results tangible - [EMPHASIS]Absolute Risk
Reduction (ARR)[/EMPHASIS]—often more clinically meaningful than relative change **Exam tip:** If a question gives only relative effects, look for baseline risk. Without baseline risk, it’s hard to judge impact. [PAUSE]
### B) Confidence Intervals: Precision and Uncertainty A [EMPHASIS]confidence interval[/EMPHASIS] tells you the range of plausible true effects. - Narrow interval: more precise - Wide interval: uncertainty, often due to
small sample size or variable results - If the interval crosses “no effect” (like RR = 1.0), evidence may be inconclusive But even if it doesn’t cross no effect, ask:
**Is the range clinically meaningful?** If a CI ranges from “tiny benefit” to “moderate benefit,” that still leaves decision uncertainty. [PAUSE] ### Mini Summary: Key Points So Far - Don’t
stop at p-values. - Prioritize effect size, absolute effects, and precision. - Translate results into patient-relevant outcomes. [PAUSE] --- ## 3) Section Two: Bias, Confounding, and Applicability—Why Good Studies Still
Mislead Even high-level evidence can mislead if there are design or execution flaws. ### A) Internal Validity: Common Bias Patterns Here are high-yield biases for exams and practice: 1. **Selection
bias:** groups differ at baseline because allocation wasn’t truly random or concealed. 2. **Performance bias:** groups receive different co-interventions, often because blinding wasn’t possible. 3. **Detection bias:** outcome assessment differs—very
relevant in dentistry where indices and radiographic interpretation can be subjective. 4. **Attrition bias:** differential dropout—especially common in long follow-ups like periodontal maintenance studies. 5. **Reporting bias:** outcomes measured but
selectively published. [PAUSE] ### B) Confounding: Especially in Observational Dentistry In cohort or case-control studies, patients aren’t randomly assigned. So confounders—like smoking, diabetes, oral hygiene, socioeconomic factors—may drive differences. If
you see an observational study claiming a strong association, ask: - Did they adjust for key confounders? - Are there unmeasured confounders? - Is the effect biologically plausible? [PAUSE] ###
C) External Validity: “Does This Apply to My Patient?” A trial might exclude patients with complex medical histories, heavy smokers, or those with poor plaque control. But those are often
your real patients. So always ask: - Are the participants similar to my patient? - Is the intervention feasible in my setting? - Are the outcomes relevant—like tooth survival, pain,
function, quality of life? [PAUSE] ### Mini Summary - Validity threats are predictable—learn the patterns. - Observational studies need careful confounder assessment. - Applicability matters as much as internal validity.
[PAUSE] --- ## 4) Section Three: Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses—Heterogeneity and “When Not to Pool” Systematic reviews are powerful, but only if the included studies are comparable. ### A) Heterogeneity:
Clinical, Methodological, Statistical [EMPHASIS]Heterogeneity[/EMPHASIS] means variation across studies. It can come from: - **Clinical differences:** different patient severity, operator skill, follow-up length - **Methodological differences:** different risk of bias, measurement
tools - **Statistical heterogeneity:** variation in effect sizes beyond chance, often summarized by [EMPHASIS]I²[/EMPHASIS] High I² doesn’t automatically invalidate a meta-analysis—but it should trigger the question: **Are these studies truly
answering the same clinical question?** [PAUSE] ### B) Fixed vs Random Effects - **Fixed effect:** assumes one true effect size; best when studies are highly similar - **Random effects:** assumes
true effects vary; more cautious when heterogeneity exists **Exam focus:** If heterogeneity is substantial, random effects are often chosen—but interpret conclusions more carefully. [PAUSE] ### C) Publication Bias Small trials
with positive results are more likely to get published. A funnel plot may hint at [EMPHASIS]publication bias[/EMPHASIS], but interpretation is imperfect—especially with few studies. [PAUSE] ### Mini Summary - Heterogeneity
is not just a statistic; it’s a clinical interpretation issue. - Pooling results can be inappropriate if studies are too different. - Publication bias can inflate apparent benefits. [PAUSE] ---

Mini Summary - GRADE is about confidence in the estimate. - Recommendations also reflect values, harms, cost, and feasibility. [PAUSE] --- ## 6) Section Five: Integrated Clinical Cases (Exam-Style Reasoning)
