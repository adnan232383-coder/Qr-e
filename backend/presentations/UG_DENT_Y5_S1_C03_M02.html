## Title: Implantology (Intro/Clinical) — Module: Clinical Applications ### Video Lecture Script (12 minutes ~1800 words) --- ## 1) Introduction & Learning Objectives Welcome to the Clinical Applications module of
Implantology. In this session, we’ll move from “what implants are” to “how we actually use them”—through practical clinical scenarios and case-based decision-making. Implant dentistry is rarely about a single perfect
plan. It’s about matching the patient’s biology, risks, expectations, anatomy, and budget to a predictable, maintainable outcome. Case studies are one of the fastest ways to build that judgement. [PAUSE]
### Learning Objectives By the end of this lecture, you should be able to: 1. Organize an implant case using a practical workflow: assessment, planning, surgery, and maintenance. 2. Identify
common clinical scenarios and choose appropriate implant strategies. 3. Recognize risk factors—like smoking, diabetes, periodontal history, and limited bone—and adjust treatment plans accordingly. 4. Apply key decision points: implant timing,
grafting needs, prosthetic design, and complication prevention. 5. Communicate options and trade-offs clearly with patients. [PAUSE] --- ## 2) A Practical Clinical Workflow: From Patient to Plan Before we jump
into cases, here’s a simple framework you can apply in almost any implant scenario. ### Step 1: Patient Assessment Think in three domains: - **Medical risk:** diabetes control, anticoagulants, bisphosphonates,
immunosuppression, smoking. - **Dental risk:** periodontal history, caries activity, parafunction, oral hygiene. - **Patient goals:** esthetics, timeline, cost, and tolerance for surgery. ### Step 2: Site Assessment Evaluate: - **Bone
volume and quality** (CBCT is often essential). - **Soft tissue phenotype**: thick vs thin biotype, keratinized tissue. - **Anatomic constraints**: inferior alveolar nerve, maxillary sinus, adjacent roots. - **Restorative space**:
occlusal clearance and emergence profile potential. ### Step 3: Restoratively Driven Planning This is a major [EMPHASIS] clinical principle: **start with the crown, then place the implant**. Plan implant position
based on: - Ideal crown position - Screw-access feasibility - Interproximal and buccolingual spacing - Emergence profile and hygiene access ### Step 4: Execution + Maintenance Surgery and prosthetics are
only part of the story. Long-term success depends on: - A cleansable design - Occlusal management - Regular peri-implant monitoring: probing, bleeding, radiographs as indicated [PAUSE] ### Quick Summary: Key
Points So Far - Use a consistent workflow: patient risk → site assessment → restoratively driven plan → maintenance. - [EMPHASIS] Restoratively driven planning prevents many “mystery failures” later. -
Maintenance is not optional; it’s part of treatment. [PAUSE] --- ## 3) Case Study 1: Single Tooth Replacement in the Esthetic Zone ### Scenario A 28-year-old patient presents with a
fractured maxillary central incisor after trauma. The tooth has a vertical fracture and requires extraction. The patient’s main concern is esthetics and avoiding a “gap.” ### Key Questions 1. Can
we place an implant immediately? 2. Do we need grafting? 3. What provisional strategy will preserve soft tissue contour? ### Clinical Reasoning In the anterior maxilla, the challenge is not
just osseointegration—it’s soft tissue stability and symmetry. Factors favoring immediate implant placement include: - Intact facial bone plate - Absence of acute infection - Thick tissue biotype - Ability to
achieve primary stability But even with an immediate implant, we often need **gap management**—commonly bone graft material in the “jumping distance” between implant and facial socket wall. ### A Predictable
Approach - Atraumatic extraction - Assess socket walls - Immediate implant if primary stability is achievable - Place graft in the facial gap if indicated - Consider a provisional restoration
or provisional abutment to support tissue form One core principle in the esthetic zone is [EMPHASIS] “position, position, position.” If you place the implant too facially, you risk recession and
show-through. Typically, you aim for a slightly palatal position with careful depth control to support an ideal emergence profile. ### Patient Communication Explain that immediate implant placement can reduce treatment
time, but esthetic outcomes depend on biology and tissue response. Also explain that a provisional tooth may be non-functional initially to protect healing. [PAUSE] ### Summary: Case 1 Key Points
- Esthetic-zone implants require meticulous planning and soft tissue management. - Immediate placement can work, but only with good socket conditions and primary stability. - [EMPHASIS] Implant position drives esthetic
success more than implant brand or surface. [PAUSE] --- ## 4) Case Study 2: Posterior Single Tooth Replacement with Limited Bone Height ### Scenario A 52-year-old patient is missing mandibular
first molar. CBCT shows limited vertical bone height above the inferior alveolar canal. The patient wants a fixed solution. ### Decision Point Do we choose: - A short implant? -
Vertical augmentation with a longer implant later? - A non-implant option like a fixed bridge? ### Clinical Reasoning Posterior mandible is load-heavy and unforgiving. The main risk is nerve injury.
Your safety margin matters. Potential strategies: 1. **Short implants**: often a practical solution when bone height is limited. 2. **Vertical augmentation**: more complex, longer healing, higher complication risk. 3. **Prosthetic
alternatives**: sometimes the best option if risk is too high. Also consider crown-to-implant ratio and occlusal scheme, especially if the patient has parafunction. ### A Practical Plan If the patient
has adequate width and bone quality, a short implant with an appropriate diameter, careful torque control, and an occlusal design that reduces overload can be predictable. Prosthetic considerations: - Narrow
occlusal table - Reduced cusp inclination - Light centric contacts, minimal excursive contacts [PAUSE] ### Summary: Case 2 Key Points - In the posterior mandible, [EMPHASIS] safety to the nerve
is paramount. - Short implants can be a predictable option in limited height cases. - Prosthetic design and occlusion are risk-management tools. [PAUSE] --- ## 5) Case Study 3: Posterior
Maxilla with Sinus Pneumatization ### Scenario A 60-year-old patient needs implants in the maxillary premolar-molar area. CBCT reveals reduced vertical bone height due to maxillary sinus pneumatization. ### Clinical Options
Common approaches include: - Crestal (internal) sinus elevation with simultaneous implant placement - Lateral window sinus augmentation with staged implant placement - Short implants as an alternative in selected cases
### How to Choose This depends primarily on residual bone height, bone quality, and desired implant length. The posterior maxilla often has softer bone, so achieving primary stability can be
more challenging. If residual height is moderate, a crestal approach may work. If residual height is severely limited, a lateral window approach may be indicated. ### Clinical Pearl Sinus cases
succeed when you respect biology: gentle membrane handling, stable graft containment, and careful post-op instructions. [PAUSE] ### Summary: Case 3 Key Points - Posterior maxilla often requires sinus-related planning. -
Approach selection depends on residual height and stability needs. - [EMPHASIS] Primary stability and membrane management strongly influence outcomes. [PAUSE] --- ## 6) Case Study 4: The Periodontally Compromised Patient
### Scenario A 55-year-old patient seeks implants due to mobility and tooth loss. History reveals generalized periodontitis with previous bone loss. Some remaining teeth show bleeding on probing and deep
pockets. ### The Core Issue Implants do not “cure” periodontal susceptibility. A patient with periodontitis history is at increased risk for [EMPHASIS] peri-implant disease. ### What You Do First Before
implant placement: - Stabilize periodontal condition - Achieve low bleeding scores - Confirm patient’s hygiene capability - Establish maintenance intervals ### Implant Planning Adjustments - Favor cleansable prosthetic contours -
Avoid deep subgingival margins that trap plaque - Consider keratinized tissue and soft tissue augmentation if hygiene access is limited - Establish baseline probing depths and radiographs after restoration ###
Communication Script (Simple and Effective) “I can place implants, but the long-term success depends on controlling inflammation. We’ll treat the gum disease first and then build a plan you can
maintain.” [PAUSE] ### Summary: Case 4 Key Points - Periodontitis history raises implant complication risk. - Control inflammation before implants. - [EMPHASIS] Maintenance is part of the treatment contract. [PAUSE]
--- ## 7) Case Study 5: The Medically Complex Patient — Diabetes and Smoking ### Scenario A 62-year-old patient wants implants. Medical history includes type 2 diabetes with variable HbA1c
and a smoking habit. The patient asks, “Can we do implants quickly?” ### Risk Assessment Two important risk modifiers: - Glycemic control: poor control increases infection risk and impairs healing
- Smoking: associated with higher implant complications and peri-implantitis risk ### Practical Approach Rather than a yes-or-no response, provide conditional planning: - Coordinate with the patient’s physician regarding diabetes control
- Encourage smoking cessation or reduction; document counseling - Consider staged treatment, conservative surgical protocols, and strict maintenance - Set expectations: longer healing time and higher risk of complications ###
Key Communication Point Your goal is informed consent: explain that success is possible, but predictability decreases with uncontrolled risk factors. [PAUSE] ### Summary: Case 5 Key Points - Diabetes and
smoking increase risk; optimization improves outcomes. - Treatment is conditional on risk management. - [EMPHASIS] Informed consent must include risk-specific discussion. [PAUSE] --- ## 8) Common Clinical Complications: What to
Watch For Let’s briefly connect these scenarios to real-world complications you’ll see. ### Early Surgical Complications - Lack of primary stability - Infection or wound dehiscence - Nerve injury (mandible)
- Sinus membrane perforation (posterior maxilla) ### Late Biological Complications - Peri-implant mucositis - [EMPHASIS] Peri-implantitis - Soft tissue recession in esthetic zone ### Prosthetic/Mechanical Complications - Screw loosening -
Fracture of ceramic or acrylic - Implant overload due to parafunction or poor occlusion - Poor cleansability leading to inflammation A consistent takeaway: many “implant problems” are planning problems—positioning, prosthetic
design, or risk assessment issues that showed up later. [PAUSE] ### Summary: Key Prevention Strategies - Plan restoratively, verify position, and respect anatomy. - Build cleansable restorations with stable occlusion.
- Monitor and maintain—bleeding, probing, radiographs when indicated. [PAUSE] --- ## 9) Putting It All Together: A Simple Decision Checklist Here’s a quick checklist you can apply to any implant
case: 1. **Is the patient medically optimized?** If not, coordinate and stabilize. 2. **Is periodontal disease controlled?** If not, treat first. 3. **Do we have adequate bone and soft tissue?**
If not, consider grafting or alternative plans. 4. **Is the implant position restoratively driven?** If not, redesign the plan. 5. **Is the final restoration cleansable and mechanically reasonable?** If not,
adjust contours and occlusion. 6. **Is maintenance scheduled and understood?** If not, clarify expectations before starting. [PAUSE] --- ## 10) Conclusion & Key Takeaways Implantology in real clinical practice is
not about memorizing one protocol—it’s about structured decision-making under variable conditions. ### Key Takeaways - Use a workflow: risk assessment → site analysis → [EMPHASIS] restoratively driven planning → execution
→ maintenance. - Match implant timing and grafting to biology, not convenience. - In the esthetic zone, soft tissue and 3D position determine success. - In posterior sites, anatomy and
occlusion are major risk drivers. - Periodontal history, smoking, and diabetes require risk modification and tighter follow-up. - Long-term outcomes depend on prosthetic design and supportive maintenance. Thank you for
joining this Clinical Applications module. In the next session, we’ll build on these foundations with more advanced planning concepts and complication management strategies. [PAUSE]
