1: SIMPLE EXTRACTION WITH A TWIST (2:30–5:00) ### Scenario A 28-year-old patient presents with intermittent pain on the lower right. Tooth #30 has a large carious lesion and is deemed
non-restorable. The patient is healthy, no medications, no allergies. They are anxious and ask, “Can you do it quickly?” [PAUSE] ### Clinical Reasoning This seems like a routine extraction—until you
remember that “simple” can become complex if you skip steps. **Assess:** - Confirm symptoms, onset, swelling, fever. - Examine: mobility, periodontal status, crown structure. - Evaluate patient anxiety—consider behavioral strategies
and local anesthesia techniques. **Investigate:** - A periapical radiograph is often sufficient. - Look for root anatomy: divergence, curvature, proximity to the mandibular canal (less relevant for first molar than
third molar, but still note). [PAUSE] ### Plan and Technique **Anesthesia:** Inferior alveolar nerve block + long buccal; confirm profound anesthesia. **Extraction strategy:** - If crown is heavily destroyed, forceps
purchase may be poor. - Consider sectioning early rather than “fighting” the tooth. A practical plan: 1. Reflect minimal soft tissue if needed for visibility. 2. Use elevators with controlled
force—support the mandible. 3. If resistance is high, section tooth into mesial and distal roots. 4. Deliver roots separately with elevators and root forceps. 5. Curette lightly if indicated, irrigate,
compress socket, assess hemostasis. [PAUSE] ### Communication and Consent Explain risks in plain language: pain, swelling, bleeding, infection, dry socket, and rare nerve effects. For mandibular molars, the nerve risk
is low but still mention altered sensation as a rare possibility. This is where you set expectations: - “You’ll have pressure, not pain.” - “Swelling is normal for 48–72 hours.”
- “Call us if bleeding doesn’t slow with pressure.” [PAUSE] ### Summary: Case 1 **Key points:** - Don’t let the word “simple” rush your assessment. - [EMPHASIS]Section early when purchase
is poor[/EMPHASIS]. - Patient anxiety management is part of clinical skill. [PAUSE] --- ## SECTION 3 — CASE 2: EXTRACTION IN A PATIENT ON ANTICOAGULANTS (5:00–7:30) ### Scenario A 67-year-old

SOCKET VS INFECTION (7:30–9:30) ### Scenario A 35-year-old returns 3 days after mandibular third molar extraction with severe pain radiating to the ear. They say it was getting better, then
suddenly worsened. They report bad taste but no swelling or fever. [PAUSE] ### Clinical Reasoning This classic timeline points toward [EMPHASIS]alveolar osteitis[/EMPHASIS]—dry socket—rather than a true infection. **Differentiate:** - **Dry
socket:** severe pain 2–5 days post-op, empty socket, exposed bone, halitosis, minimal swelling, no systemic signs. - **Infection:** swelling, pus, fever, trismus progression, lymphadenopathy, malaise. [PAUSE] ### Management Dry socket
is primarily **local care and pain control**, not routine antibiotics. Steps: 1. Gentle irrigation with saline—avoid aggressive curettage. 2. Place a medicated dressing if used in your setting (follow local
protocols). 3. Provide analgesia plan and reassure: it is self-limiting but painful. 4. Review risk factors: smoking, traumatic extraction, poor oral hygiene, oral contraceptives. If you suspect infection—systemic signs, swelling,
purulence—then antibiotics may be indicated along with drainage if needed. [PAUSE] ### Summary: Case 3 **Key points:** - Timing and symptoms guide diagnosis. - [EMPHASIS]Dry socket is not primarily an
antibiotic problem[/EMPHASIS]. - Provide empathetic reassurance and structured follow-up. [PAUSE] --- ## SECTION 5 — CASE 4: MAXILLARY EXTRACTION WITH SINUS COMMUNICATION RISK (9:30–11:10) ### Scenario A 52-year-old needs extraction
